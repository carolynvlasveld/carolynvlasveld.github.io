(2) Local volunteering opportunities:

  • Help researchers with lab and fieldwork. You can contact them directly via Twitter or university websites. Also, check these Facebook pages where they sometimes post volunteering opportunities:
    • Monash University Biological Society
    • Deakin University Ecology Volunteer Register
    They usually cover accommodation and food expenses for overnight or longer trips.
  • Some university clubs and societies run volunteer trips for activities like controlling invasive species, planting trees, and surveying flora and fauna. For example, in 2012, Monash University Biological Society ran over 25 trips around Victoria.
  • Volunteer with community groups like Landcare, Wildcare, Bushcare, Cragcare, Trackcare, or conservation initiatives like the Western Port Seagrass Partnership.
  • If you have a car, consider becoming a Wildlife Rescuer. For example, Bonorong Wildlife Sanctuary in Hobart offers training.

(3) Build confidence and safety in the outdoors:

  • Buy or hire a Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) or radio, and learn how to use it for hikes with limited phone reception.
  • Join hikes organized by Facebook groups or university outdoor clubs.
  • Learn how to use a GPS device and/or navigate with a compass.
  • Organize group hikes for friends, including easy walks (e.g., 2 km along the beach), and practice your navigation skills.
  • Be prepared for emergencies like snake bitesâ€”take a first aid course, carry a snake bandage, and bring a phone or PLB.
  • Know how to handle situations like blizzards or hypothermia by being prepared with the right clothing and survival gear.

(4) Skills and qualifications relevant to field biology and conservation jobs (or simply more ways to learn about ecology):

  • Safe chainsaw use and maintenance.
  • Four-wheel driving skills.
  • Scuba diving or free diving certification.
  • Firearms training.
  • Boat licenses.
  • First aid and CPR training. Bonus: consider Wilderness First Aid courses (highly recommended!).
